The theorem and the maths

Marden's theorem. Let $p(z) = (z-A)(z-B)(z-C)$ be a cubic with three non-collinear complex roots. The roots of $p'(z)$ are the foci of the unique inscribed ellipse tangent to the sides of $\triangle ABC$ at their midpoints — the Steiner inellipse.

Foci. Expanding $p'(z)$: $$p'(z) = 3z^2 - 2(A+B+C)\,z + (AB+AC+BC).$$ Solving gives the focal pair $$F_{1,2} = \tfrac{1}{3}\!\left(S \pm \sqrt{S^2 - 3P}\right), \quad S = A+B+C,\; P = AB+AC+BC.$$

Centre. The midpoint of the foci is $\tfrac{1}{3}(A+B+C)$ — the centroid $G$. This is forced by Vieta on $p'$: the sum of its roots is $\tfrac{2}{3}S$.

Semi-axes. Let $s = |B-A|^2 + |C-B|^2 + |A-C|^2$ and $d = A^2 + B^2 + C^2 - AB - BC - CA$ (a complex number). Then $$a = \tfrac{1}{6}\sqrt{s + 2|d|}, \qquad b = \tfrac{1}{6}\sqrt{s - 2|d|}.$$ The major axis points along $\tfrac{1}{2}\arg(d)$.

Equilateral case. $d = 0$, so $a = b$ and the inellipse is a circle (the incircle). Both foci collapse to $G$, and $p'(z)$ has a repeated root.

Useful invariants. Area of inellipse $= \tfrac{\pi}{3\sqrt{3}}\,\text{Area}(\triangle ABC)$, which is the maximum among all inscribed ellipses.
